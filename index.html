What is an option?

It's a contract between two parties so that the investor has the option to sell or buy a security in the future for a predefined price

The underlier can be any security: stocks, indeces, currencies, commodities, swaps, futures, etc.

Exchange-traded vs OTC

Exchange-traded: standardised contracts

Types: put (right to sell) or call (right to buy)

Exercise an option: execute the right

Write an option: sell the contractual right to someone

Properties

Type: put or call

Maturity/expiry: when it can be excercised

Strike price: for how much (vs: spot price: the current price)

Premium: the (upfront) price of the option

American vs European: can vs cannot be exercised before maturity

Greeks

Greeks

Greeks are different dimensions of risk involved when entering an option trade

Δ: delta

Θ: theta

Γ: gamma

ν: vega

Ρ: rho

Δ: delta

The rate of change between the option's price and a $1 change in the underlier's price

Price sensitivity of the option relative to the underlier

0 ≤ Δ(call) ≤ 1

-1 ≤ Δ(put) ≤ 0

The probability that the option will expire ITM

Delta neutral portfolio: small changes in the underlier won't affect the delta of the portfolio (option + underlier)

Θ: theta

The rate of change between the option's price and its time to maturity

Time sensitivity or time decay

The amount an option's price would decrease by as time to maturity decreases

0 ≤ Θ(long) usually

0 ≥ Θ(short) usually

Γ: gamma

The rate of change between the option's delta and its underlier's price

Δ'

The stability of an option's delta

A high gamma value means that delta can significantly change for even small underlier price changes

ν: vega

The rate of change between the option's price and the underlier's implied volatility

Implied vol: the probability of change in the security's market price (as the market views it)

The possiblity of price changes of the underlier directly affects the option's price

Ρ: rho

The rate of change between the option's price and a 1% change in interest rate

Interest rates affect economy and the markets directly (e.g. investment willingness), in turn this affects volatility, so it affects option prices

Option trading strategies

Principal-protected note

A zero-coupon bond and a call option at the same value

Guarantees that the investor will receive at least the investment

If the strike price is less than the stock price at maturity, the option won't be exercised but bond covers the call option's price

If the strike price is higher, the investor receives the option's gain + the bond's principal

An option plus its underlier

Covered call options (call option + long on the underlier)

Protective put (put option + short on the underlier)

Spreads

2+ option of the same type (put or call)

Bull spread: buy and sell the puts or calls with same underlier and maturity, but different strike prices: short call strike > long call strike or short put strike < long put strike

Bear spread: inverse of the bull spread (strike price)

Box spread: bull call spread + bear put spread with identical strike prices. It will alwyas pay off the difference between the strike prices. Only works with European options.

Butterfly spreads, calendar spreads, Diagonal spreads, etc.

Combinations

Both calls and puts of the same stock

Straddle: call and put with the same strike and maturity: pay-off is proportional to the move in the underlier's price (either direction)

Strip: 1 long call + 2 long put: underlier price more likely to decrease

Strap: 1 long put + 2 long call: underlier price more likely to increase

Strangle: a put and a call with the same maturity but different strike prices: strike price differences determine if increase or decrease means more gains

The Black-Scholes-Merton model

The Black-Scholes-Merton model

A mathematical estimation for prices of options

Developed in 1973

Won Nobel prize in 1997

For European options (can be extended to American)

The goal is to have a portfolio with the underliers to elmininate risk

Continuously revised delta hedging

Assumptions

On the assets:

  • The return rate of the risk-free asset is constant (=interest rate)
  • The returns on the risky asset follow a geometric Brownian motion
  • The risky asset does not pay dividends during the option's lifetime

On the market:

  • No arbitrage possible
  • Can lend and borrow infinie amount of money at the risk-free rate
  • The underlier has no limit to its selling (long and short)
  • There are no transaction costs

The formulae

The differential equation

BSM formula

  • c = Call premium
  • p = Put premium
  • S = Spot price of underlying asset
  • K = Exercise price
  • r = Risk free rate of return
  • T = Time remaining for expiration of option in years
  • σ = Annualized standard deviation of returns expressed as decimal
  • N = Normal distribution

How does it work?

Two parts

  • The expected benefit of acquiring the stock outright

    The stock's price * change in the call premium as the unerlier's price changes

  • The present price of paying the excercise price at maturity

Then calculate the difference between the two

Volatility arbitrage

Vol Arb

A statistical arbitrage strategy

Hedge funds like it

A lot of our clients apply it

Delta-neutral

The investor assumes they can forecast the realized vol of a security better than implied vol

The strategy

The investor uses options to bet on vol instead of price

They need to have two factors

  • The implied vol of the unerlier to calculate the option's price
  • A ("better than the market's") forecast of the vol of the underlier

If the diff is big enough, it's worth entering the vol arb

Buy the option: long on vol (realized vol will be higher than implied vol)

Write the option: short on vol (realized vol will be lower than implied vol)

Hedge the option with the underlier (so it's delta-neutral)

The investor realizes gains if the actual realized vol is closer to their forecast than to implied vol

The profit is realized continuously during re-hedging

Not a real arbitrage (no negative-cost cycle)

Why is it so difficult for us?

The client won't tell us how they implement it (they tell us the account at least)

The client won't tell us their vol predictions (that's their business model)

The strategy can work through mutliple steps (as long as it's delta-neutral)

Their vol prediction can be really bad => the portfolio can be risky

We absolutely have to make sure we charge enough according to our risk appetite and our vol prediction
